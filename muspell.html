Chapter 01

The Compliance Cliff

The Data Trap Nobody Talks About

When a hospital decides to transition thousands of staff to Epic, it's heralded as a massive modernization victory. What rarely makes the news is the logistical nightmare left behind in the server rooms: decades of patient data stranded in legacy systems like Cerner, MEDITECH, and eClinicalWorks.

Decommissioning those old mainframes is necessary to save millions in licensing fees, but doing so without a secure way to access historical patient records violates the 21st Century Cures Act. The data can't just be exported to a massive spreadsheet; it consists of complex, coded clinical histories, lab results, and legal audit trails.

Muspell was built by 314e as a B2B platform to solve this exact problem. Sold to hospital networks like Broward Health, Muspell functions as a FHIR-native archival layer. It ingests archaic formats from over 30 different legacy systems, normalizes the data, and securely surfaces it within the hospital's new, active EHR interface.

Chapter 02

Discovery & Strategy

Mapping the Unknown

I joined as the Lead Product Designer in the early planning phase of Muspell. The underlying data ingestion engine was already proving itself capable, but the surface—the interface that clinicians, HIM (Health Information Management) professionals, and compliance auditors would actually touch—was non-existent.

Early discovery phase: Mapping the chasm between raw legacy data ingestion and a usable clinical interface.

The first major challenge we encountered during our discovery workshops wasn't about UI aesthetics; it was a fundamental architectural contradiction.

"We're designing a unified view for data that inherently resists unification."

Imagine an allergy record from an obsolete 1990s system consisting solely of a free-text field matching against a fully structured, SNOMED-coded allergy record from 2018. If we forced them into a visually identical template, we ran the risk of misrepresenting the clinical certainty of the data. The source system mattered just as much as the data itself.

Strategic Decision: Transparent Provenance

I pushed to make the origin of every piece of data a first-class citizen in the UI. Instead of hiding the complexity of the legacy ingest, we exposed it as a "Source System" badge on every encounter, medication, and clinical note. For an auditor or a physician, knowing that a confusing lab result came from an outdated instance of AmbulatoryEHR1 was crucial context.

Chapter 03

User Research

Designing for the Administrator

When you think of healthcare software, you immediately visualize a physician running the halls. However, Muspell is an archival tool. The physician only uses it when they desperately need historical context that wasn't migrated. The primary user is the hospital administrator.

Synthesizing interview transcripts from HIM (Health Information Management) directors and hospital IT administrators.

I spent hours reviewing operational constraints with compliance officers and IT directors at mid-sized hospital networks. The recurring theme was fear—fear of an audit, fear of unauthorized access to a VIP patient's legacy chart, and fear of the logistical nightmare of managing permissions across merged hospital systems.

This research directly informed the system's robust configuration layer. Instead of treating settings as an afterthought, we designed them as high-assurance control panels. Administrators could govern data at a granular level, adjusting which columns appeared by default depending on whether the user was pulling a financial report or conducting an emergency room review.

Chapter 04

Visual Language

The Clinical Interface

For the clinical viewer itself, the interface needed to be deeply respectful of a clinician's cognitive load. We adopted a sparse, high-contrast, data-dense approach, utilizing IBM Plex Sans and Mono to ensure alphanumeric legibility—a critical factor when misreading a dosage number can have devastating consequences.

Our analytics showed that in archival workflows, 80% of the intent is simply finding a specific patient. There was no need for a complex homepage or an onboarding tour. We made finding the patient the absolute center of gravity for the application.

Card vs. Tabular Views

We encountered immediate friction between two core user groups. HIM staff, who rapidly audit hundreds of encounters a day, demanded tightly packed, spreadsheet-like tabular data. Medical staff evaluating a patient's historical context wanted "cards"—broader, more contextual summaries of an encounter.

Instead of compromising with a weak middle ground, we designed a robust toggle system that persisted state. A billing auditor logs in and sees dense tables; a physician logs in and interacts with rich clinical cards.

Chapter 05

Lessons Learned

What We Broke in Phase 1

The product launched successfully to its first pilot hospitals, but user feedback from the Health Information Management (HIM) departments quickly highlighted a significant oversight in my initial design strategy.

The Mistake: Underestimating the ROI Workflow

HIM professionals spend a massive portion of their day handling "Release of Information" (ROI) requests—for example, securely transmitting historical records to a law firm or an insurance provider. In our initial launch, I had scoped ROI as a basic "export" feature tucked away under a menu.

"Generating an audit certificate and a secure disclosure log isn't a peripheral task for us. It is the entire job."

— HIM Director, Pilot Hospital Network

That feedback stung, but it was incredibly clarifying. HIM departments were manually piecing together our basic export functions with external Excel trackers to manage their queues. We had inadvertently made their hardest workflow clunkier.

I immediately pivoted our focus. In the subsequent design cycles, we elevated ROI from a simple "button" to a dedicated, first-class asynchronous module. We introduced formal request queues, customizable legal forms, and automated disclosure logging. It was a stark reminder that in enterprise SaaS, failing to understand the back-office operational reality creates just as much friction as a poor UI.

Chapter 06

Business Outcomes

Scaling & Performance

Despite the early hiccups with ROI, the core archival viewer proved to be a massive operational success. We integrated directly into Epic Hyperdrive via SMART on FHIR, meaning clinicians no longer had to log into separate legacy portals. Muspell simply appeared as a native tab inside the patient's current chart.

~15 → ~11 min
Average time to retrieve complex historical records
~27%
Reduction in time spent searching per patient

In a hospital environment, shaving 4 minutes off a lookup process multiplied by hundreds of staff members translates to staggering operational efficiency.

Chapter 07

The Product Today

From Viewer to Platform

The platform live in production, processing massive volumes of clinical data daily.

Today, Muspell is a KLAS-rated platform deployed at major health systems like Broward Health, serving over 100 healthcare organizations and archiving upwards of a petabyte of patient data.

What started as a targeted interface solution for compliance has evolved deeply. It now features an AI-powered search layer, custom hospital-operations dashboards, and a FHIR-native data lake built on Apache Iceberg. While the platform has outgrown its humble phase 1 roots, the core clinical architecture and the foundational design language we established remain the bedrock of the product thousands of clinicians rely on today.
